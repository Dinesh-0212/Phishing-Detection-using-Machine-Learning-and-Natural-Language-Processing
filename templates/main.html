About

Phishing attacks have emerged as a major cybersecurity threat, tricking users into disclosing sensitive information through deceptive emails and malicious URLs. Traditional rule-based detection methods often fail to keep up with evolving attack tactics. To address this challenge, our research leverages machine learning (ML) and natural language processing (NLP) for phishing email and URL detection. We compare the effectiveness of four models—Multinomial Naïve Bayes, Logistic Regression, Voting Classifier, and Stacking Classifier—using key performance metrics such as accuracy, precision, recall, and F1-score. Our findings reveal that the Stacking Classifier, which integrates Multinomial Naïve Bayes and Logistic Regression, delivers the highest accuracy and robustness in phishing detection. By harnessing ML-driven approaches, this study demonstrates the potential of advanced techniques in strengthening cybersecurity defenses, mitigating phishing threats, and safeguarding users from online fraud.

FAQs

Q: What is phishing?
A: Phishing is a type of cyberattack where attackers impersonate a trusted entity to deceive individuals into revealing sensitive information like passwords, credit card numbers, or personal data.
Q: How does phishing typically work?
A: Phishing typically involves sending fraudulent emails, messages, or links that trick recipients into clicking malicious links or downloading malware.
Q: What should I do if I suspect an email is a phishing attempt?
A: Do not click on any links or download attachments. Verify the sender's email address, report the email to your organization or email provider, and delete the email if confirmed to be phishing.
Q: How can I protect myself from phishing attacks?
A: Use strong, unique passwords and enable multi-factor authentication. Keep software and antivirus programs updated. Avoid clicking on unknown links or downloading attachments from unverified sources.
Q: Can phishing occur outside of emails?
A: Yes, phishing can occur through SMS (smishing), voice calls (vishing), fake websites, and social media platforms.
Q: How can I protect myself from phishing attacks?
A: Use strong, unique passwords and enable multi-factor authentication. Keep software and antivirus programs updated. Avoid clicking on unknown links or downloading attachments from unverified sources. Verify the authenticity of requests for sensitive information.
Q: What are some tools to help detect phishing?
A: Anti-phishing browser extensions, email spam filters, and specialized tools like phishing detection applications.
Q: Are there any laws against phishing?
A: Yes, phishing is illegal in many countries and punishable under cybercrime laws.
Q: What should I do if I’ve fallen victim to a phishing attack?
A: Immediately change passwords for affected accounts. Contact your bank if financial details were compromised. Monitor your accounts for unauthorized activity. Report the attack to authorities or anti-phishing organizations (like APWG).
Q: How can I report phishing emails or websites?
A: Forward phishing emails to organizations like the Anti-Phishing Working Group (reportphishing@apwg.org) or your email provider (e.g., abuse@gmail.com). Many browsers also allow you to report phishing websites directly.

Guidelines

  1. Verify the sender's identity by checking the email address for discrepancies.
  2. Be cautious of emails that create a sense of urgency or fear.
  3. Look for generic greetings instead of personalized salutations.
  4. Hover over links to see the actual URL before clicking.
  5. Do not open attachments from unknown or suspicious sources.
  6. Use email filtering tools and antivirus software to detect phishing attempts.
  7. Be skeptical of unsolicited requests for personal information.
  8. Ensure websites have a valid SSL certificate (look for "https://").
  9. Regularly monitor your accounts for unauthorized activity.
  10. Trust your instincts; if something feels off, investigate further.
